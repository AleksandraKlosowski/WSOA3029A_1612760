BlogPost 1: Close Reading and Reflection

Evanthia Dimara and Charles Perin wrote an article that elaborates on what exactly the place of interactivity is within data visualization. They suggest that interactivity within this field is in fact lacking in specification. As suggested by the writers: “this confusion is due to a lack of consensual definition” (Dimara & Perin 2020, pg 1).


Ultimately what they are saying is that this lack of mutual understanding and defining of what in fact is deemed an appropriate form of interactivity in data visualization; allows for creators to create incredibly poor and lacking contributions to information. This results in meaningless interactivity that may play no real role in enhancing the data or necessarily attracting an audience through visual means. An example of this could be the highlighting of information when hovered upon with no other purpose. This would confuse the viewer into thinking that you could in fact click on the information and would make the page appear broken or bugged. It is also incredibly irritating for the viewer when information is not communicated sufficiently.


They argue that in order to tackle this dilemma; variously aged visualization researchers need to contribute and settled on a compromised understanding and definition of interactivity within this particular form of representing information. Once researchers and developers have a starting point to work from; it allows for a clearer path of exploration that will not only allow for expression and original work; however it prevents the use and implementation of meaningless, poor and irritating forms of interactivity.

BlogPost 7: Bonus Blog

I will be analysing and reflecting on Ethics in Data-Driven Visual Storytelling by Nicholas Diakopoulos. According to this article there are 3 basic principles when it comes to data driven story production. The first being to “Seek truth and report it as fully as possible.” the second being: “Be transparent.” and the third being to “Engage community as an end, rather than as a means.”


These 3 guidelines are essentially put into place in order to reaffirm the standards in data visualizations and if followed; it would prevent poorly executed work and information. Essentially he is saying that not only should data be as accurate as possible and thoroughly researched before displayed to the public, it should be done so to its fullest capacity in order to prevent misinterpretation. It is also important to be honest with the public a.k.a. “transparent”, typing in again with reporting the truth as fully as possible despite personal beliefs and motives.


Overall they break down the importance of accuracy and the significant role researches have in society due to the large access to the internet and an incredible abundance of poor information and conspiracy theories. It is our duty to present our viewers with all the information possible and therefore provide them with an aid to make the most educated and thought through decision.
